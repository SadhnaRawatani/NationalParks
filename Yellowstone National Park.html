Park Overview

Yellowstone National Park is the first and one of the most iconic national parks in the United States, established in 1872. It spans across three states: Wyoming, Montana, and Idaho, and is famous for its geothermal features, including the world-renowned Old Faithful geyser. Yellowstone is also a haven for wildlife, with bison, wolves, and grizzly bears roaming its vast landscape. The park offers a variety of outdoor activities such as hiking, wildlife viewing, and camping, and it attracts millions of visitors each year who come to explore its unique landscapes.

Wildlife in Yellowstone National Park

Yellowstone is home to a wide variety of wildlife, and its diverse ecosystems support a broad range of species. Some of the most notable species found in the park include:

  • Bison: Yellowstone is known for its large population of American bison, which can often be seen roaming the parkâs meadows and valleys.
  • Grizzly Bears: These iconic bears inhabit the park's forests and are often seen in the wild. They are an important species for ecosystem health.
  • Gray Wolves: Reintroduced to the park in the 1990s, gray wolves have become an important part of the ecosystem, helping to control the populations of elk and other herbivores.
  • Elk: Large herds of elk graze in the park, especially in the winter months when they migrate to lower elevations.
  • Trumpeter Swans: These large, elegant swans are found in the park's lakes and rivers.
  • Mountain Lions: Also known as cougars, mountain lions are elusive predators that inhabit the parkâs more remote areas.
  • Bird Species: Yellowstone is a birdwatcher's paradise with over 300 species of birds, including the bald eagle and peregrine falcon.

Yellowstone's wildlife populations play a vital role in maintaining the balance of its ecosystems, and the park is committed to their protection through various wildlife management programs.
